PROCESS DESCRIPTION
The ISBL ammonia cracking unit capacity is 12,000 tons per year of H2 product at
99.97 mol% purity (99.5 wt% H2). The process is similar for larger capacities of 24
/ 68 / 80 kTPA H2.
The unit can operate in three different modes:
• 100% NG Fuel mode where external Natural Gas is supplied to make up the
remaining firing duty, in addition to PSA Tail Gas fuel stream
• 50% NG Fuel mode where the remaining firing duty (after taking into
account PSA Tail Gas Fuel) is split, with 50% heat duty supplied by NG and
50% heat duty supplied by process cracked gas.
• 100% Cracked Gas (Clean Fuel) mode where the remaining firing duty (after
taking into account PSA Tail Gas Fuel) is supplied by process cracked gas
only. There is no direct carbon emission in this mode.
Steam requirements are balanced with steam generation from ISBL without any
external import of steams for all cases. Electricity is imported as a utility to drive
rotating equipment which are all on motor drive.

The process steps in the ammonia cracking plant are as follows:
• Ammonia liquid feed preheat, vaporization and super-heat
• Ammonia cracking
• Ammonia recovery system
• Hydrogen product purification
• Hydrogen product compression
• Steam system
• Cooling Water System
Each of these process steps and systems is described in detail in the following
sections of this document.

1. Ammonia Preheat Train
Liquid ammonia from ammonia storage tank is delivered to the ammonia cracking
unit battery limit at -33°C and 2 bar(g).
The ammonia feed, containing maximum 0.5 wt.% H O and potential trace amount
2
of oil, is pressurized to about 44 bar(g) by an ISBL feed pump through a feed drum.
The liquid ammonia feed is sent to a preheat train which is comprised of the
following steps:
• Firstly, the liquid ammonia feed is preheated against the cracker effluent
in the Liquid Ammonia Preheater 301-C to near boiling temperature.
• Secondly, more than 80% of the preheated liquid ammonia is vaporized
in the Ammonia Vaporizer 302-C by the cracker effluent, and the
remaining is vaporized in the Ammonia Start-up Vaporizer 303-C by
surplus low pressure steam from the process.
• Thirdly, vapor ammonia passes through Ammonia Feed Oil Filter 300-L
to remove any oil contaminants to prevent catalyst poisoning. The
vaporized ammonia feed is superheated in the Ammonia Cracker Feed /
Effluent Exchanger 304-C by recovering heat from the cracking furnace
effluent.
• Finally, the vaporized ammonia feed is superheated further to the
required reactor temperature by the flue gas in the convection section of
the cracking furnace.
• Start-up Electric Heater 306-C is provided for start-up heating and
vaporizing of cold ammonia (prior to availability of low pressure steam).
2. Ammonia Cracking
The preheated ammonia vapor is dissociated in the ammonia cracking section to
produce hydrogen / nitrogen mixture, following the below reaction:
(1) 2NH + heat <----> 3H + N
3 2 2
The reaction (1) is endothermic, performed under presence of catalyst with
equilibrium favored by high temperature and low pressure.

Ammonia is first cracked in the pre-cracker adiabatic reactor, before being re-
heated in the second feed pre-heat coil to further heat up for the main fired cracker.
Heat is supplied by combustion of clean fuel via down-firing burners. The ammonia
slippage at the ammonia cracking section outlet is only a couple of percent.
The ammonia cracking furnace design is based on well-proven KBR down-fired
steam methane reformer design with hundreds of references in existing commercial
ammonia plants from small to large scale capacity and decades of experience and
knowhow.
The burners are designed to use PSA tail gas as main fuel source, supplemented
by cracked gas (H /N mixture before PSA) or external natural gas as necessary.
2 2
The furnace is designed to attain maximum thermal efficiency by utilizing process
waste gases as part of the fuel, as well as by heat recovery from the flue gas in the
convection section. The flue gas, consisting of combustion products, leaves the
radiant section of the cracking furnace and enters the convection section, where its
residual heat is largely recovered by:
• Preheating ammonia vapor feed
• Generating medium pressure steam with optionally superheating
• Preheating boiler feed water
• Preheating combustion air
• Preheating PSA tail gas fuel to furnace
A motor-driven Induced Draft (ID) Fan, 301-BJ, maintains a negative draft in the
reformer radiant box, and pulls the flue gas to the stack.
Combustion air is supplied by motor-driven Forced Draft (FD) fan, 301-BJ1.
The cracking furnace is also equipped with selective catalytic reduction (SCR) beds,
therefore able to meet any NO regulation (as per client request, less than 10 ppmv
x
NO ). Ammonia solution is being used in the SCR system. It is atomized by air into
x
the flue gases prior to catalyst zone, where the ammonia reacts with NO to produce
x
nitrogen gas and water vapor.

recovering energy by preheating ammonia feed. The cooled cracker effluent is then
fed to the HP Ammonia Scrubber, 324-D, in which the cracker effluent gas is
washed by water, absorbing residual ammonia in the gas stream into the aqueous
ammonia solution leaving the bottom of 324-D.
The bottom product from 324-D is fed to Ammonia Distillation Column, 325-D, by
natural pressure difference where ammonia vapor is stripped out by heat provided
in the Ammonia Distillation Column Reboiler, 360-C. Startup pump 363-J is used
during startup when 324-D operates at lower pressure than 325-D. The overhead
vapor from 325-D is first condensed by Overhead Condensers, 362-C, and then fed
to the Ammonia Distillation Reflux Drum, 360-D.
The liquid ammonia from 360-D is pumped back to 325-D via Ammonia Distillation
Reflux Pump, 325-J/JA. A portion of liquid ammonia is recycled back the ammonia
feed preheat train.
A very small amount of uncondensed vapor from 360-D is sent to LP Ammonia
Scrubber 323-D before sending to fuel system. This minimizes the ammonia
slipping into fuel.
During normal operation, the ammonia recovery system can operate in water deficit
mode (requiring continuous water make-up) or in water excess mode (requiring
continuous water removal), depending on the water content in ammonia feedstock
and amount of slippage ammonia in HP Ammonia Scrubber inlet effluent. The plant
design & controls allow flexible operation in either case.
4. Hydrogen Product Purification
The scrubbed gas from overhead of 324-D contains 75% H , 25% N , saturated
2 2
water and less than 50 ppmv NH . A small part of the gas is split off for use as
3
supplemental fuel in the ammonia cracking furnace burners in normal operation
(clean fuel mode). The remaining majority of the gas is sent to the Pressure Swing
Adsorption (PSA) unit, 301-L.
Hydrogen purification is carried out in the Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA) unit,
301-L, to give the exit hydrogen product stream the required purity of 99.97 mole%,
whereas the rejected tail-gas stream is compressed through tail-gas compressor,
311-J, and used as fuel in the cracker furnace.
Fuel gas pre-heat coil is provided in the furnace convection section to pre-heat the
PSA tail gas fuel. Each fuel has a dedicated header and individual burner tips.

5. Steam System
The steam system of the ammonia cracking plant is shown on block flow diagram.
The header conditions are:
Temperature, °C Pressure, bar(g)
Medium Pressure (MP) Steam Header 223 23.5
Low Pressure (LP) Steam Header 173 4.0
Demin. water make-up, supplied at 5 bar(g) and ambient temperature, is fed to the
top section of Deaerator, 301-U, which is operated at 1.7 bar(g) and 130 °C. The
treated water is pumped by Boiler Feed Water (BFW) Pump, 304-J/JA, before being
preheated in BFW coil, 301-B, in the furnace convection section before being
directed to the Steam Drum 341-D, operating at 23.5 bar(g) and 223°C.
Medium pressure steam is generated under natural circulation from/to 341-D by
steam generator coil, located in the convection section of 301-B.
The main use of MP steam is to provide stripping heat to Ammonia Distillation
Column Reboiler, 360-C. Under normal operation, MP steam header pressure is
controlled by letting down to LP header or alternatively venting a small quantity of
MP steam.
The surplus LP steam is condensed in exchanger 303-C, thereby providing heat for
vaporizing ammonia feed. The steam system is designed with small water losses
from Blowdown Drum 386-D, hence demin. water make-up flow is minimal.

7. Start-up System
The start-up system is comprised of Start-Up Circulator 373-J, Start-Up Circulator
K.O. Drum 373-D, Start-Up Circulator Cooler 373-C, Start-Up Circulator Outlet
Cooler 375-C. During start-up, nitrogen is circulated by 373-J to heat up the system.
Heat is provided by natural gas to the ammonia cracking furnace.
Nitrogen circulation and heating
During start-up or upon restart from shutdown, nitrogen is circulated by 373-J to
heat up the system. Nitrogen from 373-J discharge is cooled by 375-C before
picking up heat in 304-C, pre-heat coils and cracker. Heat is provided by fuel gas
to the ammonia cracking furnace.
Catalyst reduction for Adiabatic Reactor
The adiabatic reactor catalyst requires activation by reduction process at initial
start-up and after catalyst replacement of adiabatic reactor. Currently adiabatic
reactor is reduced by passing mixture of H2 and N2 during start-up circulation at
low pressure and high temperature of ~500°C. Heating up needs to be done
gradually based on reduction procedure of catalyst manufacturer. It is possible to
procure pre-reduced catalyst for the adiabatic reactor which can reduce the re-start
time after fresh catalyst is loaded.
Catalyst reduction for Fired Cracker
Catalyst in Ammonia Cracker also requires activation by reduction process. It is
reduced by passing partially cracked gas from adiabatic reactor at low pressure and
high temperature of ~700°C.
8. Turndown & Ramp-up/Ramp-down Rate
The current confirmed single train turndown is 40% for KBR commercial units, with
minimum impact on plant performance and efficiency.
The standard ramp-up / ramp-down rate for KBR cracking plant is 30% per hour
(0.5% per minute). This rate of change is considered safe and reliable for operation
of the cracking furnace and control systems, whilst providing adequate operational
flexibility. The ramp rate can be significantly improved to 3%/min or higher if there
are project specific drivers for higher load change rates such as power generation
application.
